## What This Project Is

This project analyzes three years of Medicare claims data (2008–2010) to answer a question that matters in every healthcare organization: **who are the most expensive patients, why, and could we have seen it coming?**

The dataset — released publicly by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) — is fully synthetic, meaning it was computer-generated to mimic real Medicare patterns without containing any actual patient information. It covers roughly **116,000 beneficiaries** and includes their hospital stays, outpatient visits, and prescription drug fills.

The end result is a Tableau dashboard built on top of pre-computed, analysis-ready tables.

---

## Key Findings

### Most Medicare spending is concentrated in a small group of very sick people

The top 10% of spenders average over **$13,000/year** while the rest average a fraction of that. This isn't just a statistical quirk — it reflects a real-world pattern where a relatively small, identifiable population drives the majority of costs.

### Chronic conditions don't just add cost — they multiply it

Patients with more conditions don't cost linearly more. They cost exponentially more:

| Number of Chronic Conditions | Avg Annual Medicare Spend |
|---|---|
| 0 | $268 |
| 3 | $3,798 |
| 6 | $10,274 |
| 11 | $36,829 |

This suggests that the highest-priority intervention window is **before a patient accumulates their 3rd or 4th condition** — after that, costs accelerate sharply.

### Some conditions carry a much heavier price tag than others

Certain diagnoses add far more to annual spend than their prevalence would suggest:

| Condition | % of Patients Who Have It | Avg Extra Annual Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Stroke / TIA | 4% | +$9,980 |
| Chronic Kidney Disease | 17% | +$9,466 |
| COPD | 13% | +$8,731 |
| Cancer | 7% | +$6,859 |
| Heart Failure (CHF) | 30% | +$6,291 |
| Diabetes | 36% | +$5,778 |

Stroke affects the fewest people but creates the largest individual cost impact.

### Certain pairs of conditions together are far worse than either alone

| Condition Pair | Avg Spend (Both) | Avg Spend (Either) | Avg Spend (Neither) |
|---|---|---|---|
| CHF + COPD | $13,350 | $7,859 | $1,472 |
| COPD + Depression | $13,348 | $7,978 | $1,910 |
| Kidney Disease + Diabetes | $12,495 | $7,337 | $1,139 |

A patient with both CHF and COPD costs 9x more per year than a patient with neither.

### 1 in 14 hospital stays ends with the patient coming back within 30 days

The overall **30-day readmission rate is ~7%**. Readmissions are a key quality metric in healthcare because they are often preventable and extremely costly — the patient was just in the hospital and something went wrong.

## The Data

All data is **fully synthetic** — generated by CMS for public analytical use. No real patients are represented. The source files are available at the [CMS Data Entrepreneurs' Synthetic Public Use File (DE-SynPUF)](https://www.cms.gov/research-statistics-data-and-systems/downloadable-public-use-files/synpufs) page.

Four source files feed into the analysis:

| What It Contains | File |
|---|---|
| Hospital stays (inpatient) | `DE1_0_2008_to_2010_Inpatient_Claims_Sample_1.csv` |
| Doctor visits and outpatient care | `DE1_0_2008_to_2010_Outpatient_Claims_Sample_1.csv` |
| Prescription drug fills | `DE1_0_2008_to_2010_Prescription_Drug_Events_Sample_1.csv` |
| Patient demographics + annual spending + chronic condition flags | `DE1_0_200{8,9,10}_Beneficiary_Summary_File_Sample_1.csv` (one per year) |

Every table links back to an individual patient via a synthetic ID (`DESYNPUF_ID`). Cost fields reflect what Medicare reimbursed — not what was billed.

> **Note on 2010 data:** Average spending appears lower in 2010 than in 2008–2009. This is a known artifact of the DE-SynPUF dataset — late-arriving claims for 2010 were not fully captured in the release, so that year appears artificially lean. Trend analysis using this dataset should treat 2010 with caution.

## How the Analysis Was Built

The project runs as a sequence of six Jupyter notebooks, each building on the last.

### [01_setup.ipynb](notebooks/01_setup.ipynb) — Load everything into one place
Reads the four source CSV files into a local SQLite database (`cms_data.db`). The three annual beneficiary files are stacked into one table with a `YEAR` column added. This notebook runs once; everything else queries the database.

**Why SQLite?** The raw files total ~300MB. SQLite keeps them in a single portable file, lets every notebook query with plain SQL, and avoids spinning up an external database server for a self-contained analytical project.

### [02_eda.ipynb](notebooks/02_eda.ipynb) — Understand what you have before you analyze it
Inspects each table: row counts, data types, missing values, and cost distributions. This is standard practice before any analysis — it catches surprises (like unexpected nulls or unrealistic values) before they corrupt results downstream.

**What was found:** The data is clean. Cost columns are complete, chronic condition flags are consistently coded (1 = has condition, 2 = does not), and all tables join cleanly on the synthetic patient ID. No significant imputation or correction was needed.

### [03_cost_analysis.ipynb](notebooks/03_cost_analysis.ipynb) — Where is the money going?
Identifies the highest-cost diagnoses (ICD-9 codes), hospital procedure groups (DRGs), and patient profiles. Also computes the per-condition cost impact and segments patients into spending tiers.

**Why pre-compute condition cost impact?** Rather than showing raw averages, each condition's impact is expressed as a *delta* — the difference in average annual spending between patients who have the condition and those who don't. This controls for the fact that sicker patients accumulate multiple conditions, and makes the individual cost burden of each condition more interpretable.

### [04_readmissions.ipynb](notebooks/04_readmissions.ipynb) — Who keeps coming back?
Detects 30-day readmissions by sorting each patient's hospital stays chronologically and flagging any admission that falls within 30 days of a prior discharge. Computes readmission rates by diagnosis, by DRG, and by chronic condition status.

**Why 30 days?** The 30-day readmission window is the industry standard metric used by CMS for hospital quality reporting and Medicare reimbursement penalties. Using the same definition makes findings directly comparable to published benchmarks.

**Technical note:** Dates in the source data are stored as 8-digit integers (e.g., `20080315` for March 15, 2008). All date arithmetic in this project uses pandas, which parses this format correctly. Raw SQL date functions were not used for any readmission calculations because SQLite's `julianday()` requires `YYYY-MM-DD` format and would silently return zeros — a bug caught and corrected before export.

### [05_chronic_conditions.ipynb](notebooks/05_chronic_conditions.ipynb) — How do multiple conditions interact?
Examines what happens when patients have combinations of conditions. Computes spending by total condition count, co-occurrence rates between conditions, and the extra cost of specific high-risk pairs.

### [06_exports.ipynb](notebooks/06_exports.ipynb) — Package everything for Tableau
Pre-computes all final metrics and writes them to CSV files in `data/exports/`. Tableau is optimized for visualization, not heavy computation — by doing all aggregations in Python first, the dashboard stays fast and the logic stays auditable.

## How to Replicate This

**You will need:** Python 3.8+, the four source CSV files from CMS (linked above), and Tableau Desktop or Tableau Public.

**Step 1 — Install dependencies**
```bash
pip install -r requirements.txt
```

**Step 2 — Add the source data**

Download the DE-SynPUF Sample 1 files from CMS and place them in `data/raw/`. The expected filenames are listed in the Data section above.

**Step 3 — Run the notebooks in order**

Open Jupyter and run notebooks `01` through `06` in sequence:
```bash
jupyter notebook
```

- `01_setup.ipynb` must run first — it creates `cms_data.db` that all other notebooks depend on.
- `04_readmissions.ipynb` must run before `06_exports.ipynb` — the export notebook pulls readmission rates from the CSV files notebook 04 produces.
- Notebooks `02`, `03`, and `05` can be run in any order after `01`.

**Step 4 — Open in Tableau**

Connect Tableau to the CSV files in `data/exports/`. Each file is self-contained and ready to use as a data source. No joins are required in Tableau — all aggregations are pre-computed.

## Data Notes

- All data is **synthetic** — safe for public sharing and portfolio use.
- Beneficiary count drops slightly each year (116K → 115K → 113K) due to simulated deaths and disenrollment.
- Chronic condition flags use 1 = condition present, 2 = condition absent (not the intuitive 0/1 — handled explicitly in all calculations).
- ICD-9 codes are used throughout (not ICD-10) — the dataset predates the 2015 ICD-10 transition.
